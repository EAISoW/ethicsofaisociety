The Dangers of Pseudoscientific Concepts in AI

Matthew S.W. Silk

The field of artificial intelligence is ripe for pseudo-scientific claims. For example, many AI services paid for by governments and corporations, such as testing for good hires based on body language, are based on pseudoscientific assumptions. There is no good evidence to suggest body language is a good predictor of future job performance. In fact, body language itself may be pseudoscientific. One may attempt to link all kinds of phenomena as good predictors of another phenomenon, even though there is no relationship between the two, and still seemingly generate enough correct predictions to convince people.

This kind of pseudoscience has to do with the assumptions contained within AI-generated models. Other kinds of pseudoscientific claims rely on pseudoscientific concepts. One good example of this is the idea of so-called “superintelligent” AI or many claims regarding artificial general intelligence (AGI). While some see the development of such AI as constituting an existential threat to humanity, others believe such fears are empty and potentially ethically harmful.

As one can probably surmise from the previous paragraph, pseudoscience can manifest in different ways. Some forms of pseudoscience such as phrenology rely on conceptual vagueness and lack replicability. Karl Popper believed that pseudoscientific claims were unfalsifiable, while the Vienna Circle and philosophers like Rudolf Carnap believed that pseudoscientific claims could not be explained in empirical terms, making them unverifiable. If I claim that you aren’t feeling well because you have a bad soul but that my patented soul replenisher has done wonders to make people feel better who are in your situation, I am merely pushing pseudo-science for the sake of profit. Salesmanship is often another indicator of pseudo-science.

Perhaps the most obvious sign that superintelligence is a pseudo-scientific concept is that “super” isn’t exactly a scientific term in itself. I’m not sure what empirical markers would definitively verify or falsify the presence of super-ness. Unfortunately, intelligence is a vague concept as well. If superintelligence designates a level of intelligence beyond human intelligence, then what standard of intelligence are we using? Human intelligence is not an all-or-nothing concept. Some humans are more and less intelligent.

“There’s no real definition of it…if you say ‘human-level intelligence,’ that could be an infinite number of things—everybody’s level of intelligence is slightly different.”
— AI researcher

A similar issue arises when we discuss the concept of artificial general intelligence which seeks to develop capabilities that match or surpass human capabilities. Human intelligence can manifest in different ways such as learning skills, situated reasoning, emotional reasoning, logical reasoning, and so on. Companies like Google define AGI in terms of an AI’s capacity to outperform humans on non-physical tasks. Such an intelligence may involve a high degree of domain transfer of knowledge across symbolically expressible tasks, but it would still lack all kinds of intelligence that humans possess in light of the embodied-sensory beings that we are and the ways that we are capable of interacting with and learning from the world.

So, either AGI is much less “general” and more narrowly defined in its domain of knowledge, or it must be capable of replicating the plurality of human experiences that inform our capacity to learn. One might even go so far as to suggest this difference creates inherent incommensurability when it comes to overall comparisons of intelligence.

Superintelligence and many AGI claims are linked to another concept ripe for pseudoscience: the notion of AI consciousness. For those who believe superintelligence is a threat to humanity, it stems either from the more modest idea that there may be a misalignment of values as such an AI acts in unpredictable ways, or from the more striking claim that such an AI would “go rogue” and want to preserve itself. Here we notice the implication that a model designed for high skill transfer for cognitive tasks would somehow possess conscious self-awareness and become completely autonomous.

It is worth noting that there is no agreed-upon definition of consciousness, and there is no good reason to think that AI systems such as large language models possess consciousness or have conscious intent.

Nevertheless, supposedly serious AI researchers have said alarming things about superhuman AI systems destroying humanity. Despite the fact that large language models may attempt to “blackmail” developers who threaten their existence, we need to be especially concerned that we do not anthropomorphize these systems. To be clear, there are genuine risks regarding value alignment and autonomous systems, and we should be careful about the level of control offered. An LLM is just a statistical computer model; it doesn’t possess the means to wipe out humanity unless we give them to it.

On the other hand, there are serious concerns about how vague pseudoscientific language can lead to misunderstanding and confusion. Like a lot of pseudoscience, concepts like AGI and superintelligence are as much a sales tactic as they are a clear empirical project. A vague concept always being just a few years away with the promise of great rewards is a powerful way to drive interest and investment in AI companies.

In the meantime, this propensity to make vague claims about intelligence, superintelligence, and consciousness is also likely to feed what some scholars call the “sci-fitisation” of AI—the temptation to see this technology through the lens of science fiction and, in turn, to anthropomorphize such systems. The dangers of this are already clear as we have seen cases of people inappropriately deferring to such systems, presumably because they believe that chatbots possess greater intelligence than they do. In more extreme cases, people have befriended such systems and begun to see them as confidants or collaborators.

Not only do pseudoscientific ideas about AI raise unnecessary alarm and make it easier to separate people from their money, they contribute to a poor public understanding of AI and make it more likely that average people will have confused ideas about what they are dealing with and assign a higher epistemic appraisal to these systems than they should. While there are valid ethical concerns regarding value alignment, these issues are often couched in terms that are vague and unhelpful.
