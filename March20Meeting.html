2. AI in Education: Issues & Ideas

AI-Assisted Assessment & Cheating

  • AI enables students to fake not only writing but also typing behavior.
  • Discussion of combining AI-generated personalized exam questions with in-class written responses to ensure authentic understanding.

Grade Inflation

  • Worsening grade inflation contributes to unhealthy academic competition.
  • Suggestions included:
    • Switching to a points-based system spanning a student's entire degree.
    • More emphasis on engagement, persistence, and learning outcomes rather than pure percentages.
  • Observations that Laurier’s letter-grade system reduces grade-chasing pressures.

Motivation & Learning

  • Students increasingly focus on grades rather than learning.
  • Group suggested reforms to increase:
    • Collaboration
    • Community learning
    • Peer accountability
    • Reduction of individualistic competition

Collaborative Learning Debate

  • Some advocated for collective classroom learning models to build communication and problem-solving skills.
  • Others warned of:
    • “Classroom communism” (free-riders, uneven contribution)
    • Structural incentives that still reward individual competition
    • Systemic problems (job scarcity, societal pressures) that schools alone cannot solve

4. Model Validation, Opacity & Bias

Core Problem

AI models often appear accurate while still being wrong for hidden reasons—due to opacity, confirmation bias, and flawed assumptions.

Key Concepts

Training vs. Test Data:
Even “successful” test performance may mask underlying conceptual errors.

Confirmation Bias in AI Models:
If a model learns the wrong correlations but appears accurate on test data, users may be misled.

Feedback Loops:
Especially relevant in predictive systems (e.g., predictive policing), where model outputs actively shape the world being measured.

Predictive Policing Case Study

  • Models predict crime detection, not crime itself.
  • Finite police resources create self-fulfilling prophecies:
    • More police in one neighbourhood → more detected crime → model “validated”
    • Under-policed neighbourhoods generate no data → model never corrected
  • Hidden assumptions (e.g., infinite police resources, unbiased detection) rarely hold.

Need for Conceptual Modeling

  • The “model in the head” (human assumptions) is as important as the algorithmic model.
  • Errors arise when conceptual assumptions are never challenged.

Proposed Solutions

  • Independent data collection and external audits to break confirmation loops.
  • Multidisciplinary collaboration (philosophers, data scientists, policy experts).
  • Development of falsifiability criteria and counterfactual reasoning tools.

5. Toward AI Certification and Regulation

Certification Proposals

  • AI models as a form of “modern snake oil” needing regulation.
  • Possible creation of third-party accreditation bodies akin to:
    • Food safety inspections
    • Energy-efficiency certifications
    • Device standards compliance

Hybrid Governance Model

  • A purely governmental or purely market-driven system is insufficient.
  • Proposal:
    • Government establishes baseline standards
    • Private organizations compete to certify models
    • Public looks for “trusted AI” labels
  • This would create:
    • Profit incentives for improving certification
    • Nonprofit oversight to avoid regulatory capture

6. AI Literacy for the Public

Critical Need

  • Strong consensus: widespread AI literacy is now a societal necessity.
  • Public must understand:
    • False positives/negatives
    • Thresholds
    • Trade-offs in model accuracy
    • Limitations of AI outputs
  • Without this, people misinterpret inevitable imperfections as catastrophic failures.

Education Recommendations

  • Integrate AI literacy as early as elementary school.
  • Teach:
    • Difference between AI and non-AI tools
    • Critical evaluation of AI outputs
    • Ethical considerations
    • Proper use vs. misuse
  • Emphasize that completely refusing to use AI impedes literacy.
