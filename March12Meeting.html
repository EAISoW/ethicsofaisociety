3. Discussion Topic 1 – Facial Recognition in M&M Vending Machines

3.1 Issues Raised

Concern that vending machines on campus use undisclosed facial recognition.

Possible uses:

  • Consumer behavior analytics
  • Demographic profiling
  • Foot-traffic and churn analysis

Discovery occurred after a machine displayed a system error referencing facial recognition software.

3.2 Ethical & Security Concerns Identified

  • Lack of consent or disclosure to users.
  • Potential security vulnerabilities:
    • Wireless transmission of biometric data through unsecured channels.
  • Unclear data usage:
    • Whether images are saved.
    • Whether demographic data is sold, shared, or repurposed.
  • Slippery slope toward ubiquitous corporate surveillance, especially in semi-public spaces like universities.

Comparison to existing surveillance:

  • Many hidden campus cameras exist already.
  • Distinction drawn between surveillance for security vs. for corporate profit.

3.3 Broader Themes Raised

  • Generational differences in privacy expectations.
  • Normalization of constant monitoring.
  • Possibility of consent-based data collection in exchange for student benefits (e.g., tuition discounts — debated but controversial).
  • Equity concerns regarding accessibility to privacy and technology across cultures and countries.

4. Discussion Topic 2 – Using AI in Research Ethics Review Boards

4.1 Potential Advantages

  • Speed: Whether AI could accelerate ethics reviews.
  • Consistency: Possibility of more uniform application of principles across protocols.
  • Assistance Tool: AI as a supplementary “triage” tool or preliminary checker.

4.2 Major Concerns

  • Speed is not necessarily desirable in ethical deliberation.
  • Inconsistency within AI itself:
    • Large language models generate different outputs to identical prompts.
  • Lack of transparency:
    • Who programs or trains the model?
    • What ethical framework is embedded?
  • Human oversight still required, so efficiency gains may be minimal.
  • Risk of manipulation:
    • Researchers gaming the system by strategically phrasing proposals.
  • Bias embedded in training data:
    • Models may reproduce flawed ethical standards.
  • Cost and feasibility:
    • Building many domain-specific models could be expensive.
  • Ethical decision-making includes values, nuance, and disagreement, which are hard to encode.

4.3 Technical Inputs Shared

Discussion of:

  • Fine-tuning vs. retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
  • Multi-model voting systems
  • “Trustable AI” initiatives attempting to track provenance and data quality

Consensus: AI could be a supportive tool, but not a replacement, and significant caution is needed.

5. Discussion Topic 3 – AI and Art

5.1 Core Themes

Can AI art be considered true art?

Key ideas discussed:

  • AI cannot form intentions or choose to be creative.
  • AI art is a negotiation between artist and model rather than pure human creation.
  • Viewers are co-creators of meaning, complicating strict definitions.

5.2 Arguments Presented

In favor of AI-assisted art:

  • AI is a tool like photography, rotoscoping, or camera obscura.
  • Creativity can reside in the prompting, curation, and refinement.
  • Could democratize artistic production by lowering barriers.

Against AI as “true art”:

  • Lacks intentionality and personal expression.
  • Often acts as a derivative “copycat,” mimicking training data.
  • Threatens livelihoods in creative professions.
  • Removes the embodied, experiential process from art-making.

5.3 Middle-ground Views

AI as a collaborative tool:

  • Artist does final modification.
  • AI provides drafts, textures, or starting points.
  • Resembles historical artistic workshops where apprentices produced most of the work and masters refined or contextualized it.
